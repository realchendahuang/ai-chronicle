The GPT-4 technical report disclosed a great deal about what happened when the system was tested. It disclosed remarkably little about the system itself.

Readers could find simulated professional examinations, Olympiad problems, academic benchmarks, and comparisons before and after safety interventions. They could not find the parameter count, training-compute budget, dataset composition, or detailed architecture. The report explicitly cited competition and safety implications for the omission. A machine-learning publication had taken on a new geometry: tables of observable behavior around a sealed center.

The most portable result was the simulated bar exam, where GPT-4 scored around the top ten percent of test takers, compared with GPT-3.5 near the bottom ten percent. It was an extraordinary benchmark improvement and an easy category error. Passing questions drawn from an exam is not admission to a profession. It does not establish legal judgment, accountability, or the ability to recognize every occasion on which an answer should not be trusted.

GPT-4 remained capable of fabrication. Its errors could be harder to notice because they appeared later in a task and arrived inside more competent prose. The report measured gains in factuality and safety; it did not convert a probabilistic system into a dependable source. Prompt injection, opaque failures, and confident mistakes survived the improvement.

Image input widened the system's advertised field. Text and images could take part in one interaction, allowing questions about diagrams, documents, and visual scenes. Yet the multimodal capability entered access gradually. At launch, image input was limited, while text through ChatGPT and the API became the production reality for most users. The demos marked a direction before they marked a universally available product.

Why close the technical center while expanding the behavioral perimeter? By March 2023, frontier models were no longer only contributions to a research literature. ChatGPT had made them strategic products. Training details had commercial value; release created abuse questions at enormous scale. OpenAI continued to publish evaluation and safety work, but the report's purpose was now partly to establish trust in a service without permitting outsiders to reconstruct the service's core.

This is not simply a story of secrecy replacing science. The disclosed evaluations provided evidence worth examining, and disclosure of a recipe has never guaranteed reproducibility at frontier cost. Still, the asymmetry became a defining fact of the period. The public could call the model repeatedly, institutions could build around it, and researchers could probe its outputs, while the process that produced those outputs remained beyond public inspection.

The report left outside researchers in a new position. They could probe an API, design harder evaluations, and compare failures across repeated calls, but they could not independently reconstruct the model's scale, data mixture, architecture, or training run. Reproducibility had shifted away from rebuilding the machine and toward interrogating a service whose center remained closed.
