Model-family names once looked like a building with clear floors: the largest model on top, costliest, slowest, presumed smartest; smaller models below, trading ability for speed and price. Claude 3.5 Sonnet began to warp that floor plan.

When Anthropic released it on 20 June 2024, API pricing stayed in the Sonnet tier—$3 per million input tokens and $15 per million output tokens, the same tier as Claude 3 Sonnet—with a 200K-token context window, while comparison targets pointed at the previous flagship, Claude 3 Opus. The company said it ran at roughly twice Opus's speed and published gains on reasoning, knowledge, vision, and coding evaluations. On an internal agentic coding evaluation, Anthropic reported Sonnet solving 64% of tasks versus 38% for Claude 3 Opus. Those figures show how Anthropic scored its own model; test sets, tool configuration, and grading set their borders. They do not replace comparison on real workloads and are not independent third-party replications.

Pricing Sonnet against prior Opus is both technical claim and price psychology: stronger, still mid-tier invoice. For startups and high-frequency agent loops, that combo decides ship-or-not more than occasional top-flagship calls. Vision and code on the same page show mid-2024 “primary models” were expected to handle screenshots and repos. Artifacts pulled Claude.ai toward iterable work objects. Mid-tier name, primary-model reality—the day's clearest product sentence.

Leaderboards explain only part of the release. Once large models enter production, capability is never a single top score. How long a request waits, how many calls fit in a day, whether long documents fit, whether code edits execute stably—all enter the final choice. A strongest model too expensive for anything but occasional consultation rarely becomes a workflow base. Claude 3.5 Sonnet put what Anthropic called flagship-level performance into more practical latency and cost, making “primary model” a more useful label than “largest model.” It shipped on Claude.ai and the API, including free-tier access under product limits—availability as a usable workhorse, not an enterprise-only flagship trial.

Artifacts, introduced the same day, gave the change a product shape. Generated code, documents, or web pages no longer sank only into the chat log; they appeared in a side surface that could be inspected and edited. Chat output was treated as a work object, not merely a message. The interface did not automatically grant stronger reasoning, but it changed the relation between user and result: talk on one side, watch something take form on the other; revise the artifact rather than hunt the previous version in a long thread. Artifacts entered Claude.ai as a preview capability—bound to the model launch day, belonging to a different product layer.

Claude 3.5 Sonnet therefore sat at two turns. On the supply side, vendors began improving the whole curve of ability, speed, and price rather than fighting only at the costliest end. In the interface, answers gradually became objects to operate, save, and iterate.

Model grades did not vanish. Flagships still stretch toward harder tasks; small models still chase efficiency. From this Sonnet generation on, product teams asked less often “which SKU is largest” and more often a daily question: which one am I willing to leave on? Answering by parameter count or family number alone misses the choice people actually made in summer 2024—latency, unit price, context, and whether a piece of work can finish.
