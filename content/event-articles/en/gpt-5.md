GPT-4 and o1 had taught users to make a technical choice before asking an ordinary question. One family answered quickly; the other spent more time on difficult reasoning. Names, latency, and price appeared in the menu, and the user had to guess which kind of computation the problem deserved.

GPT-5 moved that guess into the product. OpenAI joined quick response, deeper reasoning, coding, and tool use within one family, with routing intended to allocate more computation when the task required it. The shorter menu concealed a longer series of decisions: estimate difficulty, choose a reasoning depth, determine whether a tool should be called, and balance reliability against delay and cost.

Routing distributes compute unevenly by design. Many requests remain fast; others earn a longer process. The unification belongs chiefly to the experience and model family. It does not describe one immutable model doing identical work on every prompt.

For developers, that made a single family available across immediate interaction, hard reasoning, and agentic workflows. The relevant outcome was no longer only whether a response sounded good on its first turn. It was whether the system could select tools, carry the task through, and finish at an acceptable cost. Intelligence at the product layer increasingly included the decision about how much intelligence to purchase for this particular request.

The model choice did not vanish when the menu receded. It changed hands. Users were spared a switch, while the platform acquired more authority over a process they could no longer inspect merely by reading the selected model name. Convenience and opacity were part of the same exchange.

GPT-5's product mechanism can be stated without claiming that the model family settled anything final about intelligence. The user specified the work. The system decided how long to spend, whether to reach for tools, and how much computation the work was allowed to consume. One less button concealed one more judgment.
