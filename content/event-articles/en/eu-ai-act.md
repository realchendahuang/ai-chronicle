On 12 July 2024, Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 appeared in the *Official Journal of the European Union*. Publication gave a long legislative argument the settled density of law: numbered articles, defined roles, prohibited practices, documentation duties, enforcement, and dates that did not all point to the same day.

The regulation does not impose one uniform condition on everything marketed as artificial intelligence. It sorts by risk and by use. Certain practices judged unacceptable are prohibited, including specified forms of manipulation, exploitation of vulnerability, social scoring, and biometric use under defined conditions. Particular systems used in areas such as employment, education, medicine, critical infrastructure, and law enforcement may fall into high-risk categories. Those systems face connected requirements for risk management, data governance, technical documentation, logging, transparency, human oversight, accuracy, and cybersecurity.

Many ordinary applications do not become high-risk merely because they use a model. Some systems instead carry limited transparency obligations; others remain outside the demanding tiers. The distinctions matter because legislation must name who owes what to whom. “AI risk” is not an actor capable of keeping a log or answering an enforcement request.

Generative models spread quickly while the text was being made, forcing another layer into the scheme. The Act assigns duties to providers of general-purpose AI models and adds stricter requirements for models classified as posing systemic risk, including evaluation, risk mitigation, and incident reporting. It also addresses matters such as copyright policy and publication of summaries about training content. Upstream model providers and downstream deployers do not bear identical obligations, but neither can assume that responsibility disappears in the handoff between them.

The result reads slowly because a product and a statute answer different kinds of question. A launch demonstrates what a system can do now. Law must consider intended purpose, reasonably foreseeable misuse, placement on the market, monitoring across a lifecycle, avenues for complaint, and the evidence available after harm. The AI Act did not descend in full when the Journal was published. It entered into force twenty days later, while prohibitions, general-purpose-model rules, and high-risk obligations followed their own phased timetables. “Europe regulated AI” names a construction project lasting years, not a switch thrown in July.

Its reach also refuses to stop neatly at the Union's border. Providers established elsewhere may come within scope when systems or their outputs enter the European market and affect its users. Companies are thus pressed to translate the vocabulary of voluntary principles into operating facts: which category applies, where training and validation data came from, what records exist, how human oversight can intervene, and whether limitations appear where users can actually encounter them.

The regulation may prove burdensome in places, late in others, and difficult to interpret as both standards and technologies change. Those disputes will belong to its history. Its immediate effect was more prosaic. Risk could no longer remain only a final slide in a presentation called “Safety.” It had to acquire an owner, a file, a deadline, and sometimes a name that could be held responsible.

Models answer in milliseconds. A legal order moves article by article. Slowness is not wisdom by itself; here it is the pace left by writing responsibility down one duty at a time.
