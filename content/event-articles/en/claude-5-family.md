In June 2026, Anthropic’s model table took two entries inside one month. On 9 June, Claude Fable 5 became the broadly released top tier, positioned for long-running, difficult agent work; on 30 June, Claude Sonnet 5 took over the balanced workhorse tier, stressing speed and intelligence together. The dates must stay separate: a family “taking shape” is a process, not one press day. Misremembering the end-of-June map as an early-June finish scrambles who arrived when.

Layering is itself a product claim. Fable points at longer execution, larger output budgets, and harder agent jobs; Sonnet points at the daily default—strong enough, fast enough, used enough. Performance claims in launch materials remained vendor-reported at first release, not independently reproduced conclusions. Copying positioning words into a capability certificate confuses marketing language with a benchmark protocol. Real acceptance still asks whether the task finished, how errors recover, and whether cost is bearable.

From rapid 4.x Opus / Sonnet / Haiku iteration to a 5-series that redraws roles with Fable and Sonnet, the competition axis shifts: not only single-turn scores, but whether complex work can be finished and sustained. Coding agents, professional workflows, and long tool loops share one family story. Names change; acceptance questions do not. When users hit the default key, they are betting on latency, bills, and failure rates.

The shape of the Claude 5 family at the end of June is a map that will still move. Its value is telling users which tier to click by default; its risk is implying that layers already equal measured capability marks. Marks must be drawn again on real workloads—and drawn separately for Fable’s long runs and Sonnet’s daily work.

The Fable–Sonnet split also rewrites who “default Claude” is in users’ heads. Long-run jobs hit Fable; daily writing and coding hit Sonnet—if market positioning takes, tutorials and scaffolds will quickly harden the habit. Rename after hardening and migration cost appears. A family taking shape is not only a model-table update; it is a reallocation of the default key.

Three weeks between the June releases were enough for early Fable users to form habits before Sonnet’s default split arrived. Habits plus split are what make a family map “take shape.” After that, the next rename is no longer only marketing—it is migration.
