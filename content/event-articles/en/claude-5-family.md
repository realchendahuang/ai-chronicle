The Claude 5 family did not appear neatly in one launch.

On 9 June 2026, Anthropic first released Fable 5 and Mythos 5, which share the same underlying model. Fable was for general use with conservative real-time guardrails: some cybersecurity topics were routed instead to Claude Opus 4.8. Anthropic said such interceptions averaged under 5% of sessions and admitted harmless requests would sometimes be caught. Mythos 5 lifted some of those limits and was initially available only through Project Glasswing, a U.S. government collaboration, to a small set of cyber defenders and infrastructure institutions. Same intelligence, different admission rules—the key to early Claude 5 history: flagship capability is not the same permission set for everyone.

Glasswing and Mythos make “who may use near-ungated high capability” a public policy question. Fable's live interception publishes false-positive cost: safety taxes useful requests. Sonnet 5 defaults and effort curves continue 3.5-era economics: adjustable compute pushes mid-tier into hard tasks. Same-day BrowseComp chart correction is rare self-exposure—curves depend on protocol. Claude 5's chronicle interleaves capability, price, and access, not a single upgrade line.

Three days later, Anthropic paused access to Fable 5 and Mythos 5. The update said restoration was underway without covering the outage in a victory narrative. On 1 July both models returned. The family's first page of history therefore carries a crease: release, withdrawal, redeployment. For a model sold partly on long autonomous work, availability is itself part of capability; service interruption cuts across multi-day task stories.

Anthropic positioned Fable 5 as the highest-capability tier then widely offered. The company said advantages over older models grew with longer, harder tasks; official and early-partner materials cited large code migrations, long-horizon games, knowledge work, and life-science research. Anthropic also claimed attention across multi-million-token long tasks and improvement via the model's own written file notes. Those materials show how the vendor was defining “stronger”: not only one answer, but whether a model can keep a plan, use tools, and continue after failure. Case studies, partner quotes, and vendor evaluations remain launch materials—not substitutes for independent verification in other environments.

Sonnet 5 arrived on 30 June with a different job. It did not try to hand the highest capability to everyone; it pressed some agent work that once needed Opus-class models into the primary price band. Anthropic made it the default for Free and Pro and put it in Claude Code and the API. Official curves showed that, on some agent search and computer-use tasks, raising effort could approach Opus 4.8 at lower price. The word “some” matters: the same page said Sonnet 5's cybersecurity capability was substantially below current Opus models. Capability borders did not vanish because family codes changed.

The Sonnet 5 page even revised a BrowseComp cost–performance chart the same day. A simpler method had understated performance; the revised figure used a system-card protocol with a 10M-token budget, context compression, and programmatic tool calling. Benchmark charts are not natural scales growing from the model; budgets, scaffolding, and tools move the curve. Leaving the revision on the page is more valuable than an orphaned claim of “near Opus.”

Claude's tiers were no longer the familiar Opus–Sonnet–Haiku size order. Fable stood for widely available but tightly guarded high capability; Mythos for trusted access to the same underlying model; Sonnet for the mainline work between price and performance. More names also recorded more complex product arrangements. Frontier models are hard to line up on a single small-to-large ladder: how long they can work, what they are allowed to do, who may use them, and how much a task costs all help define “which model this is.” Reading Claude 5 as “another stronger Sonnet/Opus” misses what the family actually wrote into the chronicle: capability and access split into parallel product dimensions.
