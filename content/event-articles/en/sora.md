When Sora appeared on 15 February 2024, people could not join a queue or send it their own prompts. OpenAI released a research preview: curated videos, a technical note, and plans for limited access to red-teamers, visual artists, designers, and filmmakers. It was not a consumer product launch and not a day-one public API.

That distinction decides what launch materials can support. Samples showed text-to-video up to about one minute, image-to-video, and extension or completion of existing clips. Against common few-second text-to-video of the time, length, clarity, and subject continuity looked striking. Public pages showed results the team chose to display, not the distribution independent users would get under arbitrary prompts. They prove the model can achieve those cases, not that it always can, and not any average success rate.

“World simulator” is research ambition language and should be stored separately from curated clips. Official failure types—missing bite marks, illogical glass, flipped spatial relations, reversed causality—are harder materials than ads. One-minute length forces later video models to compete on temporal consistency, not only texture. Safety, watermarking, and C2PA plans show photoreal video carried authentication and abuse issues from day one; issues named in preview are stress-tested in product.

The technical note offered a larger framing than “make the picture move.” Video and images are compressed into a latent space and cut into spacetime patches; a diffusion transformer processes those patches the way a language model processes tokens. Quality improved with data scale, compute, and model size. One representation could hold visual data of different resolutions, aspect ratios, and durations, so OpenAI described the route as a path toward a “world simulator.”

The name attracts and requires distance. Generating continuous frames is not the same as holding reliable physics inside. Official materials listed failures: a bitten cookie without a bite mark; glass breaking illogically; spatial relations flipping; cause and effect reversing. Sora is skilled at continuing visual statistics and therefore skilled at building a world that is locally plausible and globally unplaceable. The prettier the shot, the later some errors become visible.

One minute also changes the temporal nature of video generation. A few seconds need only sustain the surface of one action; longer shots require people, background, occlusion, and motion to remember one another across more frames. The demos show a diffusion transformer can stretch that memory; they do not erase identity drift, object permanence, or complex motion failures. The frontier moves outward, and defects move from “doesn't look right” to “the world is inconsistent with itself.”

Outside the preview, questions the samples alone could not answer remained: where training video came from, how copyright and creator compensation would work, how photoreal generation would be labeled, how person impersonation and misinformation would be limited, who gains efficiency in film work and who bears displacement pressure. OpenAI mentioned safety testing, detectors, and C2PA metadata plans—vendor schemes and commitments at the time, to be tested after product access. Collapsing the February research page with later, gradually widened product access erases the temporal structure of “show first, open later.”

Sora's launch page is a travel album from an unstable world. Each shot can make a viewer pause. After closing the page, one should still remember that travelers were not yet allowed to go themselves, and that the album did not include every time the model got lost.
