There was no queue to join when Sora first appeared. The public encounter on 15 February 2024 consisted of selected videos, a research note, and a plan to give limited access to red-team researchers, visual artists, designers, and filmmakers. It was a viewing, not yet a trial.

That distinction controls the evidence. The clips showed videos generated from text at lengths of up to one minute. Sora could animate a still image and could extend, fill in, or connect existing footage. Against the brief and visibly unstable outputs common to earlier text-to-video systems, the duration, clarity, and continuity were startling. Yet the web page contained results chosen by the team. It established that the system had produced those cases; it did not describe the distribution an independent user would receive across unselected prompts and repeated attempts.

The accompanying explanation proposed something larger than making pictures move. Images and videos were compressed into a latent representation and divided into spacetime patches. A diffusion transformer processed those patches in a manner analogous to the tokens handled by a language model. The same representation could accommodate different resolutions, aspect ratios, and durations. OpenAI reported better generation as data, computation, and model scale increased, and described the approach as a path toward “world simulators.”

The phrase was irresistible because film already invites belief in a world beyond the frame. It also asked for more caution than the images encouraged. Continuity is not the same as a reliable internal physics. The launch materials displayed failures: a bitten cookie might remain whole; glass could break in an implausible way; spatial relationships might change; cause and effect could reverse. Sora could continue the visual statistics of a scene while losing the rules that would make the scene inhabitable.

A minute altered the nature of the difficulty. A few seconds can preserve the appearance of one movement. A longer shot requires subjects, backgrounds, occlusion, and motion to remember one another over many more frames. The preview showed that diffusion transformers could sustain that memory farther than before. Identity drift, object permanence, and complex dynamics did not disappear; their errors simply occurred inside footage polished enough that a viewer might notice them late.

Beyond the catalogue were questions the catalogue could not answer: the provenance and licensing of training video, compensation for creators, identification of synthetic footage, impersonation, misinformation, and the redistribution of work in film and advertising. OpenAI described plans for safety testing, detection, and C2PA metadata. At that point, these were provider measures and commitments awaiting examination when the system reached broader use.

Sora's first page can be read as an atlas of a country not yet open to travelers. Its landscapes were vivid, and some roads ran for an unprecedented length. But the publisher chose every photograph. The atlas did not show how often the road folded into itself, nor permit the reader to choose a destination and test whether it existed.
