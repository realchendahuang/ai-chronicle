Alan Turing did not begin *Computing Machinery and Intelligence* by answering its famous question. He began by declining its terms.

“Can machines think?” asks two undefined words to carry more weight than they can bear. A definition of *machine* might be managed, though even that category was changing quickly in 1950. A definition of *thinking* would summon ordinary usage, theology, philosophy, and human vanity before any experiment could begin. Taking a poll on the meaning of the words, Turing observed, would be absurd. So he proposed a different question, not as a synonym for the first but as a way out of its grammar.

The original imitation game is stranger than the classroom diagram it later became. An interrogator communicates by text with a man and a woman and tries to determine which is which while one participant attempts to mislead. Turing then asks what would happen if a machine took one participant's place. The textual channel is crucial. It removes the machine's body, voice, surface, and materials from consideration. No points are awarded for synthetic skin or a human silhouette. What remains is conduct under a shared condition: answers, evasions, mistakes, style.

That subtraction gives the game both its force and its severe limit. Passing it would not reveal whether a machine had experiences like ours. It would not establish consciousness or deliver a definition of intelligence. The game takes an inaccessible noun and replaces it with an organized encounter whose result can be observed, repeated, disputed, and redesigned. The later fame of the “Turing test” has often obscured how much Turing chose not to claim.

The paper itself ranges far beyond the game. Turing considers objections from religion, mathematics, consciousness, disability, and originality. He separates conjecture from result. His often-quoted forecast imagined that, in about fifty years, an average interrogator after five minutes of questioning would have no better than a 70 percent chance of making the correct identification. The percentage is less durable than another proposal near the paper's end: instead of attempting to program an adult mind rule by rule, build a “child machine” and educate it. Learning, reward, punishment, language, and experiment might accomplish what direct construction could not. The teacher might not fully understand the internal changes produced by the lessons.

The objections are not arranged merely so that Turing can knock them down. To the claim that a machine can do only what it is ordered to do, he does not deny that a program follows rules; he asks whether its designers can always foresee the consequences of a sufficiently elaborate program. Mathematical limits are conceded, then placed beside human fallibility. The standard is repeatedly made symmetrical. A limitation does not become uniquely disqualifying just because it has been demonstrated in a machine.

Turing thus made two substitutions. He exchanged *What is thought?* for *What difference can an interrogator detect?* Then he exchanged *How do we write intelligence?* for *How might a machine acquire its behavior?* The first produced a public ritual that has never stopped attracting contestants. The second quietly altered the engineering problem.

The imitation game did not decide whether a machine could possess a mind. Its more lasting demand is less theatrical: whenever someone claims to have recognized intelligence, they must say what, exactly, they were able to observe.
