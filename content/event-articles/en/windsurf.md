Before Windsurf, Codeium was easily summarized as a free option for AI code completion. That position brought many users and constrained the imagination around the product. Completion appeared after the cursor, but it rarely made the provider the center of a developer's workday.

On 13 November 2024, Codeium launched the Windsurf editor. It did not abandon completion. It placed completion, chat, repository-scale changes, and a system called Cascade inside a standalone editor, with multi-step flows occupying the main interface. The model could maintain context around the current codebase, propose changes, and continue into later steps. Codeium was no longer competing only to be a Copilot alternative. It wanted to own the environment where the developer stayed all day. That repositioning changed the opponent. Cursor had already demonstrated that rebuilding AI interaction on top of a familiar editor ecosystem could form an independent category. Windsurf gave the market more than one conspicuous example. Developers could compare how products gathered context, displayed changes across files, and moved between completion and agent flows. Competition expanded from model response speed to the rhythm of the entire workflow.

Cascade's “flow” was naturally demonstrable because it placed consecutive actions on the screen. Production use raised less glamorous questions. Which files did a flow inspect? Where did it resume after a failure? How could a user understand and reverse changes spread across locations? Completion allowed line-by-line acceptance. A multi-step agent postponed review until the change set was larger. The smoother the interface appeared, the more clearly it needed to reveal breaks and boundaries.

Windsurf also remained continuous with Codeium's earlier business. Completion was still a frequent entry point and a basic source of perceived speed. Cascade tried to extend occasional suggestion into sustained collaboration. Whether movement between the two felt natural would determine if “AI-native” described a new product structure or only a new story wrapped around existing features.

The launch mattered less as a declaration of who had won than as the beginning of real horizontal comparison among AI editors. There was no longer only one board on the wave. Developers could vote through migration, context quality, review experience, and price. Every switch would force a product to prove that it offered more than another sidebar capable of conversation.
