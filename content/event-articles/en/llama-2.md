On 18 July 2023, Meta released Llama 2 pretrained and chat fine-tuned weights at 7B, 13B, and 70B, free for research and commercial use. The second half of the sentence must be read with the first: terms are the Llama 2 Community License, with an acceptable-use policy and additional limits such as a monthly-active-user threshold—not unrestricted OSI open-source software. Microsoft is the preferred cloud partner, not the only lawful deployment path. Free to use is not blank terms.

First-generation LLaMA sat between a noncommercial research license and a grey toolchain after the leak. Llama 2 tried to move downloadable checkpoints into product contracts: base and Chat variants shipped together; the paper described longer training, stronger safety fine-tuning, and red-teaming. Weights came by request; clouds hosted them. Terms still barred uses such as improving other large models with outputs, and very large commercial use needed separate permission—openness stratified. Lawyers and engineers had to read the same model note at one table.

For enterprises, private deployment and self-hosted services could rest on official checkpoints rather than only closed APIs. For the community, fine-tuning and quantization continued, but full license text had to be read. For industry language, open weights, open code, and open-source licenses could no longer be mixed without challenge. Marketing pages said “open”; compliance pages said “additional terms”; readers need both.

Llama 2 did not end the argument over “true open source.” It only drew the border more clearly: weights that can enter a contract, and every additional term on the license, must appear in the same day’s account. Write only half, and it is publicity, not history.

Cloud hosting and local deployment heated up together. Enterprises could place weights in private environments; communities continued fine-tunes and quantization; lawyers watched MAU thresholds and acceptable-use policies. Llama 2 pushed commercially usable open weights into the mainstream—and pushed extra terms into the mainstream with them. From then on, a download button needed a readable license summary beside it; “open” without that summary is only marketing language.

Acceptable-use policy and MAU thresholds pushed open weights into contracts and into compliance checklists together. Download is light; finishing the terms is not. Llama 2’s historical place sits between those poles: more open than fully closed, more restrained than unrestricted open source.

Microsoft as preferred cloud partner sped enterprise access and should not be read as the only lawful home. Weights may still deploy elsewhere under the license. Open-weight politics often run between who shelves first and who writes terms; Llama 2 took a seat at both tables.
